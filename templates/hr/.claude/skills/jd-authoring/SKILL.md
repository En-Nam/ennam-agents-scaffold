# JD authoring playbook

A good JD describes the work and the bar — not a wish list. Use this skill any time you draft a new JD or review one before it goes live.

## Structure (in this order)

1. **Summary** — 2-3 sentences: what the role exists to do, who it reports to, why it matters now.
2. **Responsibilities** — 5-8 outcome-oriented bullets ("Ship X", "Own Y"), not task lists.
3. **Required (must-have)** — strict day-1 needs only. If the person can learn it in the first 90 days, it is NOT must-have.
4. **Preferred (nice-to-have)** — everything that helps but isn't blocking.
5. **Competencies** — 4-6 named competencies with one-line definitions. These drive the interview kit.
6. **Compensation** — band from the user, or `[BAND PLACEHOLDER]`. Never invent numbers.
7. **Benefits** — pulled from the company handbook, or `[BENEFITS PLACEHOLDER]`.

## Must-have vs nice-to-have discipline

Ask of every requirement: *"Would we reject an otherwise excellent candidate who lacked this on day 1?"*
- Yes -> Required.
- No -> Preferred.

Long Required lists shrink the candidate pool and skew demographics. Keep it tight.

## Gender-neutral language checklist

- Swap "rockstar", "ninja", "guru", "he/she" -> "engineer", "specialist", "they".
- Prefer "collaborative" over "aggressive", "driven" over "dominant".
- Cut superlatives that read as gatekeeping ("world-class", "elite") unless they're concretely defined.
- Run the text mentally past someone outside the target demographic — would they self-select out?

## EEOC / fairness guardrails

Never include or imply:
- Age, generation, "digital native", "recent graduate" (unless it's a structured early-career program).
- Family status, marital status, pregnancy.
- National origin, citizenship beyond legal work authorization.
- Religion, political affiliation.
- Disability or health status.

## Sign-off

Mark the file `DRAFT` at the top until a human approves. Surface every placeholder and every judgment call in your handoff message.
